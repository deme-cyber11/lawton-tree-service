How to Choose a Tree Service Contractor

March 1, 2026 · 4 min read

Hiring a tree service contractor is a significant decision. Whether you need tree removal, trimming, or emergency storm cleanup, choosing the wrong company can result in property damage, injury, or financial loss.

Unfortunately, the tree service industry has its share of unqualified operators, fly-by-night companies, and unlicensed contractors. As a homeowner in Lawton, OK, it's important to know what to look for and what red flags to avoid.

Here's a practical guide to choosing a reliable, professional tree service contractor in Lawton.

1. Verify Licensing and Insurance

This is the single most important step. A legitimate tree service company must carry proper licensing and insurance to protect both you and their workers.

What to ask for:

  • General Liability Insurance: This covers property damage if something goes wrong during the job (e.g., a tree falls on your house or fence).
  • Workers' Compensation Insurance: This protects you from liability if a worker is injured on your property.
  • License to Operate: While Oklahoma doesn't require a state-level tree service license, reputable companies often hold certifications from organizations like the International Society of Arboriculture (ISA).

Don't just take their word for it. Ask to see proof of insurance and verify it directly with the insurance company. If a contractor hesitates or refuses, walk away.

2. Get Multiple Written Estimates

Never hire the first tree service you call. Get at least 2-3 written estimates to compare pricing, services, and professionalism.

A good estimate should include:

  • A detailed description of the work to be done
  • Cost breakdown (labor, equipment, hauling, etc.)
  • Timeline for completion
  • Cleanup and disposal details
  • Payment terms

Be wary of estimates that are significantly lower than others. Extremely low bids often indicate corner-cutting, lack of insurance, or hidden fees that will appear later.

3. Ask for References and Check Reviews

A reputable tree service company should have a track record of satisfied customers. Ask for references from recent jobs, and actually call them. Ask about:

  • Quality of work
  • Professionalism and communication
  • Cleanup and site condition after the job
  • Whether the final cost matched the estimate

Also check online reviews on Google, Facebook, and the Better Business Bureau (BBB). Look for patterns in feedback. One bad review isn't a dealbreaker, but multiple complaints about the same issue (e.g., poor cleanup, hidden fees) are red flags.

4. Look for Proper Equipment and Safety Gear

Professional tree service companies invest in high-quality equipment and prioritize safety. When evaluating a contractor, look for:

  • Professional-grade chainsaws, rigging equipment, and chippers
  • Bucket trucks or cranes for tall trees
  • Personal protective equipment (PPE) like helmets, gloves, safety glasses, and harnesses
  • Well-maintained vehicles and tools

If a contractor shows up with rusty equipment, no safety gear, or a beat-up pickup truck with a ladder in the back, that's a major warning sign. Tree work is dangerous, and professionals take safety seriously.

5. Beware of Red Flags

Some warning signs indicate you're dealing with an unreliable or unqualified contractor. Avoid companies that:

Go Door-to-Door Soliciting Work

Reputable tree service companies don't need to knock on doors looking for work. Storm chasers and scammers often canvass neighborhoods after severe weather, offering "great deals" that turn into disasters.

Demand Cash Payment Only

Legitimate businesses accept checks, credit cards, or digital payments. Cash-only operations are often unlicensed and uninsured, and you'll have no recourse if something goes wrong.

Refuse to Provide a Written Estimate

A verbal estimate isn't worth the air it's spoken into. Without a written contract, you have no protection if the price suddenly doubles or the work isn't completed as promised.

Ask for Full Payment Upfront

Standard practice is to pay a deposit (often 25-50%) upfront and the balance upon completion. Any contractor demanding full payment before starting work is a major risk.

Can't Provide Proof of Insurance

If they can't or won't show you their insurance certificate, don't hire them. Period. You could be held liable for injuries or property damage.

6. Ask About Certifications and Experience

While not required, certifications from professional organizations demonstrate a commitment to quality and safety. Look for:

  • ISA Certified Arborist: A credential from the International Society of Arboriculture, showing the arborist has passed rigorous exams and maintains continuing education.
  • Tree Care Industry Association (TCIA) Accreditation: A mark of professionalism and adherence to industry best practices.
  • Years of Experience: Ask how long the company has been in business and serving the Lawton area.

Experience matters, especially in Oklahoma where severe weather can create unique challenges for tree care.
